# Limitations

This repository is a controlled research workbench. Its conclusions are
deliberately narrow, and the boundaries below are part of the result, not
footnotes to it. Read [RESULTS.md](RESULTS.md) and [FINDINGS.md](FINDINGS.md) only
within these limits.

## The benchmarks are synthetic

Every benchmark is generated by code, not drawn from real corpora. Items, queries,
relevance labels, timestamps, and source-quality signals are all manufactured to
isolate one context decision at a time. The naturalistic families
(`email-old-signal`, `meeting-action-items`, `support-stale-fix`,
`revision-current-truth`, `memory-log-noisy`) are *shaped* like real working
information — threads, revisions, stale fixes — but they are still synthetic
constructions with known ground truth, not samples of real inboxes, tickets, or
logs. Numbers transfer to other synthetic instances of the same generator, not to
real-world data.

## The generators are deterministic and simple

Cases are produced from a seed by small, transparent generators. This buys exact
reproducibility (see [docs/reproducibility.md](docs/reproducibility.md)) at the
cost of realism: distributions are simple, vocabularies are small, distractors are
constructed rather than naturally occurring, and the structure a strategy must
exploit is cleaner than it would be in the wild. A strategy that wins here has
matched the generator's assumptions; that is weaker than matching reality.

## Oracles are ceilings, not achievable targets

Every `oracle*` strategy reads ground-truth relevance, facts, or value that a
deployable system cannot see. They exist to provide an upper bound and to express
results as a *gap to the best possible*. An oracle number is never evidence that a
real method can reach it, and oracle strategies must never be read as
recommendations.

## No real-world or production claims

This project makes **no** claim that any strategy will help a production system,
a specific model, or a real retrieval pipeline. There is:

- no evaluation on real datasets,
- no human evaluation,
- no latency, cost, or throughput measurement,
- no integration with any model, vector store, or serving stack,
- no statistical significance testing beyond small multi-seed means.

The strategies named as strong (e.g. `salience-retention`,
`retention->selection`) are strong *on these benchmarks*; the robustness study
(Phase 10) already shows several of them collapsing when the signal they trust is
corrupted. Treat every finding as a hypothesis to be re-tested on real data, not a
deployment guide.

## Measurement caveats

- **Few seeds.** Means are over seeds `{1, 2, 3}`; variance estimates are coarse
  and there is no significance testing.
- **Mixed metrics across benchmarks.** Each benchmark has its own primary metric,
  so cross-benchmark aggregates in the synthesis (Phase 9) mix metrics; the
  strongest comparisons are *within* a benchmark.
- **Budgets compared by number.** Budget sweeps compare by numeric limit even
  though units differ (items vs tokens) between families.
- **Mechanical failure flags.** The failure modes in synthesis are threshold
  checks, not root-cause analyses.
- **Perturbations are illustrative.** The five perturbations stress specific
  assumptions (distractors, contradictions, stale repetition, corrupted
  source/salience signals); they are not an exhaustive adversary model.

## Scope exclusions by design

The repository intentionally contains no external APIs, no LLM calls, no network
access, no dashboards, notebooks, or web applications. These are not missing
features; they are out of scope so that results stay deterministic and auditable.
See [docs/non-goals.md](docs/non-goals.md).
