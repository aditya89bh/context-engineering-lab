# Phase 9 summary: cross-benchmark synthesis

Phase 9 steps back from running experiments and instead **synthesises** the
artifacts the Phase 2-8 suites already produce. It answers a different kind of
question — not "how does strategy X do on benchmark Y?" but "what does the whole
body of results say, taken together?" — without adding any new strategy,
benchmark, metric, or algorithm.

## What Phase 9 adds

- A `synthesis` package that loads and validates result artifacts, discovers them
  recursively, and aggregates per-seed metric values into one cell per
  `(benchmark, strategy, metric, budget)` with a metric-orientation table.
- **Strategy profiles**: each strategy's strongest and weakest benchmarks, best
  and worst budgets, and mean distance to the oracle ceiling.
- **Dominance analysis**: pairwise wins/losses/ties over shared cells and the
  non-dominated (Pareto) frontier.
- **Oracle-gap analysis**: per-cell gaps to the oracle and an oracle-normalized
  performance score per strategy.
- **Failure analysis**: mechanical flags for budget collapse, wide oracle gaps,
  and budget-driven metric degradation.
- **Stability analysis**: seed variance, budget sensitivity, and ranking
  volatility across budgets.
- A deterministic Markdown report and a `context-lab run-phase9` command that
  re-runs the suites and writes both the artifacts and the synthesis.

See [synthesis.md](synthesis.md) for the methodology.

## What it does *not* claim

- No new behaviour is measured; synthesis only re-describes existing numbers.
- Conclusions are specific to the synthetic benchmarks, seeds, and budgets behind
  the artifacts — not claims about real-world context systems.
- Primary metrics differ by benchmark, so cross-benchmark comparisons mix metrics
  and are strongest within a benchmark.
- `oracle` strategies are ceilings, not deployable approaches.
- Failure flags are threshold checks, not root-cause explanations.

## Benchmark-specific reading

The synthesis reflects, but does not overturn, the per-phase findings: oracle
ceilings stay at or near the top of every dominance and normalized table;
importance-aware strategies (`salience-retention`, `retention->selection`) sit
closest to their oracles across the families they run on; budget-ignoring
references (`retain-all`) can exceed a budgeted oracle on a single metric and so
show a negative oracle distance; and strategies evaluated on disjoint benchmarks
never compare, so the non-dominated frontier is wide by construction. The numbers
behind these statements are regenerated by `context-lab run-phase9`.
